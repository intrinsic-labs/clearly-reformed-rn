If God Is Sovereign, Then Why Should I Pray?

Kevin DeYoung  ·  Jun 18, 2026  ·  8 min read

Of all the objections to prayer, the one that presses hardest on thoughtful Christians is not whether God can hear us, but whether our asking changes anything at all. If God has already ordained whatsoever comes to pass, what room is left for petition?

Scripture never asks us to choose between God's sovereignty and the meaningfulness of prayer. It holds the two together without apology. The same Lord who declares the end from the beginning also commands his people to ask, to seek, and to knock.

Two truths, held together

Prayer is not the lever by which we move God to our will. It is the means God has appointed by which he accomplishes his will through the asking of his people. The end and the means are ordained together, and neither one cancels the other.

We do not pray because the outcome is uncertain. We pray because God has made our asking part of how the certain outcome comes to pass.

This is why the most sovereignty-minded saints have so often been the most prayerful. The doctrine that some imagine must surely kill prayer has, in the lives of those who actually believed it, done the opposite — it has fueled it.

So pray. Not as those who must inform God, nor as those who must wear him down, but as children speaking to a Father who delights to give good gifts, and who has woven your small voice into the unshakable purpose of his will.
